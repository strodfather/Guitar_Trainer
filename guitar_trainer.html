Harmonic Functions

Every chord in a key has a function — a dramatic role in the story of tension and resolution.

Tonic (I)
Home base. Sounds complete and resolved. Everything in music is moving toward or away from this point.
Subdominant (IV)
Moves away from home — restless but not urgent. Creates contrast without the strong pull of the dominant.
Dominant (V)
Maximum tension. Built on the 5th degree. A dominant 7 chord contains a tritone that pulls powerfully back to tonic.

The I-IV-V is the foundation of blues, rock, and country. In A: A (I) · D (IV) · E (V). Your minor pentatonic in A works over all three.

The ii-V-I is the jazz engine. In C: Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7. The ii prepares the dominant, the dominant resolves to tonic. Understanding this progression lets you target chord tones through the changes.

Perfect intervals: A Perfect 5th (7 semitones) is always stable — the power chord interval, never clashes. A Perfect 4th (5 semitones) is open and strong, but can clash over a major I chord because it sits a semitone above the major 3rd. Use the 4th to pass through rather than land on over a major tonic chord.

Color toneStable scale notes that add flavour without clashing over the chord.
TensionNotes a semitone from a chord tone — they create energy and want to resolve.

Interval Reference

An interval is the distance between two notes counted in semitones. One fret = one semitone. The labels R, b3, 5, b7 tell you exactly how far each note sits from the root of the key.
